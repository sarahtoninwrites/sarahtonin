Yoyo Mama Jokes and Objective Morality

Sarah Saiyed

I have no professional qualifications to write an essay about objective morality. There is no philosophy degree in my pocket, nor all that many non-fiction books on my shelf. However, just hear me out. No, hold on, I’m sure I can come up with a better reason for you to stay for this essay. *subtly offers a large sum of money.* (I'm an engineering student with no source of income)

Anyways, my knowledge of objective morality comes from contemporary fiction, tv shows, Reddit threads, and most importantly, cue the confetti cannons- personal experience. (Of course, I also did research on the subject while writing this, references will be credited at the end.)

To understand objective morality, allow me to copy and paste the definition of morality from the very helpful www.betterhelp.com “morality is often a belief about what is wrong and right regarding our behavior, beliefs, and words.”
Morality is what is believed to be right or wrong- stealing is bad, helping others is good, your mom is not so big that when she walks past the TV you miss entire seasons of a show, etc.

Objective morality takes this a step further to say- something can only ever be right or wrong. It sits on the cushion of absolute morality. Stealing is always bad, helping others is always good and your mother should never be subject to criticisms about her size. But what if- the person stealing is a poor boy with no other means of obtaining sustenance? Or maybe, the person you’re helping is a bank robber? Maybe, your mom is obese and requires medical attention (not in the form of yo mama jokes, but you get the point I'm making-). Objective morality leaves no room for gray areas and no room for white lies.

You might’ve heard the term ‘morally gray’ thrown around in pop culture. Morally gray actions are those we have difficulty classifying as right or wrong because they may be both and neither at the same time. As ‘Better Help’ describes, “Lying isn't often considered a moral action. However, there could be instances where lying would cause the least harm and may feel just.“

Characters in pop culture are often described as morally gray when their actions have us ask, ‘Does the end justify the means?’. While that question is a whole conversation in itself, an example of this would be Eren Yaeger. (AOT SPOILERS AHEAD || SKIP TO THE NEXT PARAGRAPH) To save his people, Yaeger had to bring about mass genocide. His actions were the reason the conflict in his story ended but at the cost of billions of innocent lives. Did the end (end of war and the salvation of his people) justify the means(genocide)? Were his actions ‘absolutely good’ because he just wanted to save people? Or, was he ‘absolutely bad’ because he killed people in the process? Hence the term, morally gray.

Objective morality paints a very black-and-white picture- either right or wrong, no gray. This immediately deters me because, of course, things can’t always be as straightforward as that. Objective morality holds no room for context, history, or perspective. It’s a very utopian idea- and there are theories that if morality is objective, we can bring out the ideal society.

In a society where morality is objective- yo mama jokes are always bad, no matter how funny they may be; you cannot tell any lies to spare someone from a hurtful truth, and helping people (rob banks) lands you straight into heaven. Lit, innit?

It sounds dandy until you realize this means the requirement for action will never justify the action, nor will the intention with which it is performed. For example, murder would be murder, regardless of whether it was committed with malicious intent or as an act of self-defense.

If not objective morality, then what- subjective morality? Subjective morality should then mean that we, as individuals, create our own ‘right’ and ‘wrong’; we decide what goes and what doesn't. As Better Help puts it, “Subjective morality says morals are human-made and can vary from person to person.”

However, if everyone had their morality with no consensus, we would live in utter chaos. I say I’m a fantastic person because I helped someone rob a bank, you say I’m a shitty person because they robbed other people’s money, and the court of law would, of course, would not exist. Therefore, it is up to individuals to resolve the disputes- but my verdict says I’m innocent, and yours says I’m guilty- but neither of us is right nor wrong because, by subjective morality, both of us are entitled to our values. This means that I, nor anyone committing a crime and claiming to not be guilty, will ever be guilty of anything at all.

To solve this predicament, it seems logical to say, ‘Hey who’s got the best moral compass? Follow in their footsteps.’ Sounds plausible when you first think of it, but- this person (Karl Wray, Carlisle, Cumbria) gives some very bitter food for thought, “Certainly many people have attempted to invent their own morality and then impose it on others, for instance, Stalin, Hitler, Mao Zedong, and Pol Pot. And look what they created – a new Humanity, a new Society! Does anyone fancy living in their morally subjective worlds? Each led to inhumanity and madness.”

Where does this bring us? Back to square one? If objective morality is unreasonable and subjective morality leads to too many disparities, can we make yo mama jokes?
